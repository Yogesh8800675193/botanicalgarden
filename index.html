History Of Indian Botanical Garden

Introduction

The Acharya Jagadish Chandra Bose Indian Botanic Garden, formerly known as the Indian Botanic Garden, is a historic botanical garden located in Shibpur, Howrah, near Kolkata, West Bengal. Established in 1787 by Colonel Robert Kyd, an officer of the British East India Company, the garden is one of the oldest and most important botanical gardens in India. Spanning over 273 acres, the garden is renowned for its extensive collection of more than 12,000 plant species, including rare and exotic varieties. It serves as a hub for botanical research, conservation, and education, making significant contributions to the study of Indian flora since its inception.

Among its notable features is the Great Banyan Tree, a sprawling tree estimated to be over 250 years old, which holds the distinction of having one of the world’s largest canopies, covering over 4 acres. The garden also houses the Central National Herbarium, which contains over 2.5 million plant specimens, making it one of the largest plant repositories globally. The garden's early focus on economic botany and the acclimatization of foreign plants, such as tea from China, helped shape India's agricultural landscape. Today, under the administration of the Botanical Survey of India, the garden stands as a testament to India's rich botanical heritage and ongoing efforts in plant conservation and research. It is a destination for scientists, students, and nature lovers alike, offering a glimpse into the diversity and richness of the plant world.

Visiting hours

Open daily from 8 AM to 5:30 PM (March to September) and 8 AM to 5 PM (October to February). Closed on Mondays.

Establishment

1787: The garden was established by Colonel Robert Kyd, an officer of the British East India Company, with the aim of identifying plants of commercial value and developing a sustainable source of timber, especially teak, which was in great demand for shipbuilding.

Early contribution and focus

The garden initially focused on economic botany, including the acclimatization and cultivation of plants from other regions. It became instrumental in introducing and cultivating several exotic plants, including tea from China, which later became a major crop in India. It also played a significant role in the study of Indian flora, with botanists documenting a wide variety of native plants and trees.

Role in botanical search

The garden became a center for botanical research under the superintendence of William Roxburgh in the early 19th century, who is often considered the Father of Indian Botany. Roxburgh made extensive contributions to the documentation of Indian flora, and many species discovered during this time were named after him. The garden’s herbarium, known as the Central National Herbarium, is among the largest in the world, housing over 2.5 million specimens.

Key Features and Notable Trees

Great Banyan Tree: One of the garden's most famous attractions is the Great Banyan Tree (Ficus benghalensis), which is estimated to be over 250 years old. It has a canopy that spreads over 4 acres, supported by thousands of aerial roots, and is often considered the world's largest tree in terms of its canopy coverage. The garden is home to more than 12,000 plant species, including both native and exotic varieties, such as palm trees, orchids, cacti, and medicinal plants.

Contribution to Tea Cultivation

During the 19th century, the garden played a crucial role in the introduction and successful cultivation of tea in India, particularly in regions like Assam. This helped establish India as a major tea producer globally.

Renaming and Recent Developments

In 2009, the garden was renamed the Acharya Jagadish Chandra Bose Indian Botanic Garden to honor the renowned Indian scientist who made significant contributions to botany and plant physiology.
Today, the garden is under the administration of the Botanical Survey of India and serves as a major center for botanical research, conservation, and education.

Most Visited Botanical Garden In India

Acharya Jagadish Chandra Bose Indian Botanic Garden (Howrah, West Bengal)

Established:- 1787

Famous For:- The Great Banyan Tree, one of the largest in the world.

Key Features:- Over 12,000 plant species, including rare and exotic plants.

Importance:- India’s oldest and largest botanical garden, pivotal in introducing tea and cinchona plants​

Lalbagh Botanical Garden (Bengaluru, Karnataka)

Established:- 1760

Famous For: The Glass House inspired by London's Crystal Palace.

Key Features: 240 acres with a diverse collection of tropical plants, and biannual flower shows.

Importance: Major center for plant conservation and horticultural research.

Tropical Botanic Garden and Research Institute(Thiruvananthapuram, Kerala)

Established:- 1979

Famous For: Focus on conservation of tropical plants.

Key Features: Over 50,000 accessions of tropical species, including medicinal plants and bamboos.

Importance: Dedicated to research and conservation of Western Ghats flora.

National Botanical Research Institute (Lucknow, Uttar Pradesh)

Established: 1800 (under Nawab Saadat Ali Khan)

Famous For: Extensive research in plant sciences and biodiversity.

Key Features: Over 10,000 species, specializing in plant taxonomy and breeding.

Importance: A major hub for research under the Council of Scientific and Industrial Research (CSIR).

Botanical Garden of the Forest Research Institute (Dehradun, Uttarakhand)

Established:- 1906

Famous For: Extensive research in forestry and wood sciences.

Key Features: The garden hosts a variety of conifers, bamboos, and medicinal plants.

Importance: It’s a major center for forest-related research and plant biodiversity in northern India.

Government Botanical Garden (Ooty, Tamil Nadu)

Established:- 1848

Famous For: Rare species of ferns, orchids, and a fossilized tree trunk over 20 million years old.

Key Features: Spread across 55 acres, known for its annual flower show.

Importance: A leading tourist attraction and home to several rare and endangered plant species​.
